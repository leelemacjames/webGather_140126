The Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, at the eyepiece

A non-parametric test in two acts: a maximum vertical gap between curves, and what that gap is allowed to mean. Worked through with two globular clusters in mind.

§01 Why this test, why astronomy

When two physicists compare measurements they often start by comparing means. The mean is a strong summary, but it is a single number standing in for a whole distribution. A KS test refuses that compression: it compares the whole shape of two samples against each other, asking whether they could plausibly have been drawn from the same parent population.

That property is precious in astronomy. Stellar masses follow a power law (the IMF). Galaxy luminosities follow a Schechter function. Globular cluster luminosities are roughly Gaussian on a log scale. Almost nothing in the sky is naturally Gaussian on a linear scale, which means tests that assume Gaussianity are tests against a fiction. The KS test makes no such assumption — it is non-parametric — and so it travels freely between subfields.

Before reading on: if two globular clusters have the same mean stellar magnitude but very different spreads, would a t-test notice?
Reveal · the answer

No, not directly. A standard t-test asks specifically whether two means differ. Two distributions with identical means but wildly different shapes will pass a t-test untroubled. The KS test, comparing CDFs, sees the difference instantly because the curves diverge in the wings even when they cross at the centre.

§02 The empirical CDF, geometrically

Take a sample of measurements — say, the V-band magnitudes of N stars in a cluster. Sort them small to large. The empirical cumulative distribution function is the step function that climbs by 1/N at each measured value. By the time you reach the largest measurement, the function has reached 1.

This object is the spine of the whole method. It is built directly from the data with no smoothing, no binning, no assumed model. Two samples produce two such staircases, and the KS test simply asks: at the point where these staircases disagree most, how far apart are they?

§03 The statistic D

D = supx | FA(x) − FB(x) |

The supremum is just "the maximum, taken carefully". Among all real numbers x, find the one where the gap between the two CDFs is widest. That gap is D. Because both curves run from 0 to 1, D itself lives between 0 and 1: a value of 0 means the staircases coincide everywhere; a value near 1 means the samples occupy completely different parts of the line.

A p-value can be computed from D and the two sample sizes. It answers a single, precise question: if the two samples really did come from the same parent, how surprising would it be to see a gap this large? If the answer is "very", we doubt the same-parent hypothesis. The conventional cut is p < 0.05; in astronomy we are usually more demanding.

Confusion box · what the p-value is not

The p-value is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true. It is the probability of seeing data this extreme given the null. Examiners and reviewers both like to catch this distinction; it is worth memorising the precise wording.

Reveal · what to expect

The t-test will typically return a large p-value: it cannot detect a difference because the means are equal by construction. The KS test will return a small p-value: the CDFs diverge in the tails even though they cross at the centre. This is the canonical example of why "compare the means" is not the same as "compare the populations".

§05 Versus the parametric world

Parametric tests — t, z, chi-squared, F — assume a particular distribution (usually Gaussian) and exploit that assumption to extract more information from each data point. When the assumption is correct, they are more powerful: they detect real differences with smaller samples.

When the assumption is wrong, they are not just inefficient; they can be misleading, returning small p-values for the wrong reason. The KS test surrenders some power in exchange for the right to remain silent about the underlying distribution. In a field where you are often unsure of the parent distribution — every astrophysical sample is incomplete in some way — that trade is usually worth it.

A short comparison

For two cluster magnitude samples, in rough order of usefulness:

Welch's t-test

Compares means, allowing for unequal variances. Powerful if both samples are approximately Gaussian. Blind to differences in shape, skew, or tails. Useful only when you trust the Gaussian assumption.

Mann–Whitney U / Wilcoxon rank-sum

Non-parametric like KS, but tests whether one distribution is stochastically shifted with respect to the other. More powerful than KS for detecting shifts, less sensitive to differences in shape.

Anderson–Darling

A close relative of KS that weights deviations in the tails more heavily. For astronomical applications — where the tails often carry the rare, interesting populations — Feigelson & Babu explicitly recommend AD over KS. See §06.

Chi-squared goodness-of-fit

Compares binned counts to a model. Sensitive to bin choice, which is a known weakness. Strong when you have a parametric model with definite predictions and many counts per bin.

§06 The well-known caveats

The KS test is so easy to apply that it has become, in Feigelson & Babu's phrase, the most over-used statistical test in astronomy. The major caveats — worth knowing even before you run your first one:

1. The KS test is most sensitive at the median, weakest in the tails. A difference confined to the rarest 5% of objects may not register, even when it is the difference that matters scientifically.

2. The standard p-value is invalid if you estimate the parent distribution from the data. If you fit a Gaussian to your sample and then KS-test the sample against your fitted Gaussian, the tabulated p-values are wrong (the Lilliefors correction or a bootstrap is required).

3. The two-dimensional KS test is non-trivial. The version published by Peacock (1983) and refined by Fasano & Franceschini is widely used in astronomy but is not strictly distribution-free; bootstrap calibration is recommended.

4. With very large samples, the KS test will reject almost any pair of populations. Real-world data are never exactly from the same parent. With N = 10⁶, trivial differences become statistically significant. Always look at D itself, not just the p-value.

Confusion box · what astronomers actually do now

For univariate tests, many astronomers have shifted to the Anderson–Darling test as a default, with bootstrap calibration if the parent is fitted from the data. The KS test remains useful as a quick visual diagnostic — its statistic D has a transparent geometric meaning the AD statistic does not.

§07 Two clusters, one question

A worked situation, in the spirit of an IB Physics extended response. Two globular clusters in the Galactic halo, NGC X and NGC Y, have stellar V-band magnitudes measured from HST photometry. You suspect — from prior metallicity work — that they might belong to the same accreted dwarf galaxy and therefore share a stellar population.

The samples: NGC X has 320 stars with magnitudes ranging from 14.2 to 18.9. NGC Y has 280 stars with magnitudes ranging from 14.5 to 19.2. The means differ by 0.18 mag, the standard deviations by 0.05 mag.

Without running anything: what does the KS test answer that a comparison of means cannot? And what scientific conclusion would you draw if KS returned p = 0.42?
Reveal · the framing of the answer

A means-comparison answers only "are the typical brightnesses the same?". The KS test answers "are these two populations consistent with being drawn from the same parent at any magnitude?" — including the bright tail (which would be most informative for the giant branch) and the faint tail (which probes the lower main sequence).

A KS p-value of 0.42 does not prove the populations are the same; it merely fails to reject the same-parent hypothesis. It is consistent with shared origin but does not establish it. The next move is to look at metallicity distributions, kinematics, and ages — which would either reinforce or break the inferred kinship. This is the typical workflow in halo-substructure papers.

What you might do in code

The Python is brief. Given two arrays magsX and magsY:

from scipy import stats
D, p = stats.ks_2samp(magsX, magsY)
A2, crit, sig = stats.anderson_ksamp([magsX, magsY])
print(f"KS: D={D:.3f}, p={p:.4f}")
print(f"Anderson–Darling: A²={A2:.3f}, p≈{sig:.4f}")

Running both, and reporting both, is good practice. If they agree, your conclusion is robust. If they disagree, that disagreement is itself a finding — typically pointing to a tail-confined difference where AD wins.

§08 References & further reading

Selected entry points, ordered roughly from teaching-friendly to research-level. The asterisks mark where to start.

  1. ★ Feigelson & Babu. "Beware the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test!" Astrostatistics and Astroinformatics Portal, Penn State. essay
    asaip.psu.edu/articles/beware-the-kolmogorov-smirnov-test
    The standard short critique. A genuinely accessible read; covers the major caveats in plain English. Read this first.
  2. ★ Feigelson & Babu (2012). Modern Statistical Methods for Astronomy with R Applications. Cambridge University Press. textbook
    The standard reference text in astrostatistics. The KS material sits in the goodness-of-fit chapter; the chapter on non-parametric methods is a strong primer. Code in R but trivially translated.
  3. Babu & Feigelson (2006). "Astrostatistics: Goodness-of-Fit and All That!" ASP Conference Series 351. conference
    The technical companion to the "Beware" essay. Includes the bootstrap-correction prescription that has become standard in careful work.
  4. ★ Di Cecco et al. (2010). "A detailed study of the main sequence of the globular cluster NGC 6397." Astronomy & Astrophysics 512, A48. globular cluster
    aanda.org · 10.1051/0004-6361/200912516
    An exemplary use of the KS test on a globular cluster luminosity function. Synthetic populations versus observed; clean and accessible. Excellent worked example.
  5. Sanchez Almeida & Asensio Ramos (2010). "Properties of stellar generations in globular clusters and relations with global parameters." Astronomy & Astrophysics 519, A85. globular cluster
    aanda.org · luminosity function comparisons
    Uses the KS test to compare luminosity functions of inner-halo, outer-halo, and dSph globular clusters. The kind of population-comparison reasoning that maps directly onto the §07 setup above.
  6. Sollima & Nipoti (2010). "Globular clusters in modified Newtonian dynamics: Palomar 14." arXiv:0911.4485. globular cluster
    arxiv.org/abs/0911.4485
    A nice cautionary case: a KS test applied to only 17 stars in Palomar 14 cannot strongly falsify MOND, despite earlier claims. A teaching gift on sample-size limitations.
  7. Metchev & Grindlay (2002). "A two-dimensional Kolmogorov–Smirnov test for crowded field source detection: ROSAT sources in NGC 6397." MNRAS 335, 73. globular cluster · 2D KS
    arxiv.org/abs/astro-ph/0204391
    How the 2D KS test gets used on real X-ray imaging of a globular cluster core. Shows the method beyond textbook examples; introduces the Peacock / Fasano-Franceschini machinery.
  8. Tosi et al. (2022). "Robust statistical tools for identifying multiple stellar populations in globular clusters: a case study of NGC 2808." arXiv:2110.08269. globular cluster
    arxiv.org/abs/2110.08269
    Modern application: clustering and KS tests applied to the multiple-population problem in NGC 2808. Shows where KS is one tool among several.
  9. Harrison, Sutton, Carvalho & Hobson (2015). "Validation of Bayesian posterior distributions using a multidimensional Kolmogorov–Smirnov test." MNRAS 451, 2610. advanced
    arxiv.org/abs/1404.7735
    For the keen: extends KS to the multidimensional Bayesian-validation setting. Beyond IB, but a good signpost for where the method is taken in modern cosmology.
  10. Press, Teukolsky, Vetterling & Flannery. Numerical Recipes (any edition). §14.3.3. classic
    The classic numerical-implementation discussion of KS, including the small-sample p-value formula. Old-fashioned but lucid.
